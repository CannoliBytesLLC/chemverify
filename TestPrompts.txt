While  is inherently capable of reducing both aldehydes and ketones, literature precedents demonstrate that exceptional selectivity for aldehydes over ketones can be achieved by modulating reaction conditions such as temperature and solvent composition (Ward & Rhee, 1989).

Typically, these reductions are conducted in protic media—most commonly **methanol** or **ethanol**—which play a critical role in activating the carbonyl group through hydrogen bonding and assisting in the dissolution of the borohydride salt (Robinson & De Jesus, 1996; Chaudhuri et al., 2010). Kinetic studies have established a clear reactivity hierarchy where **aliphatic aldehydes > aromatic aldehydes > ketones > conjugated enones** (Ward & Rhee, 1989). For instance, performing the reaction at low temperatures (e.g., **°C**) in a mixed solvent system of alcohol and dichloromethane allows for the nearly exclusive reduction of aldehydes in the presence of more sterically hindered or electronically deactivated ketones (Ward & Rhee, 1989).

Beyond standard homogeneous conditions, recent methodologies have introduced solid-state variants, such as using ** supported on wet silica gel**, which facilitates rapid, solvent-free reductions at room temperature with high yields (Zeynizadeh & Behyar, 2005). These advancements underscore the enduring utility of borohydride-based reagents in modern synthetic programs, providing a reliable pathway to alcohols while tolerating a wide array of other sensitive functional groups including esters, nitriles, and nitro groups (Ward & Rhee, 1989; Lamm et al., 2013).

# Test 2: Multistep Synthesis of Benzocaine from *p*-Nitrotoluene

Since you're interested in carbonyl chemistry and literature-supported procedures, a classic and highly pedagogical multistep sequence is the **synthesis of Benzocaine** (ethyl 4-aminobenzoate) from *p*-nitrotoluene. This sequence demonstrates oxidation, esterification, and reduction.

---

## Multistep Synthesis of Benzocaine

### Step 1: Oxidation of *p*-Nitrotoluene to *p*-Nitrobenzoic Acid

The methyl group of *p*-nitrotoluene is oxidized using a strong oxidant, typically potassium permanganate.

* **Procedure:** To a mixture of *p*-nitrotoluene (1.37 g, 10 mmol) and Na₂CO₃ (0.5 g) in 40 mL of water, KMnO₄ (4.74 g, 30 mmol) is added. The mixture is refluxed for 2 hours until the purple color disappears and a brown MnO₂ precipitate forms. After filtration and acidification with c.HCl, the white carboxylic acid precipitate is collected.
* **Reference:** *Vogel's Textbook of Practical Organic Chemistry*, 5th Ed., Section 6.13.
* **DOI:** [10.1021/ed056p526](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://doi.org/10.1021/ed056p526) (Note: This DOI discusses variations in oxidation techniques).

---

### Step 2: Fischer Esterification to Ethyl 4-Nitrobenzoate

The carboxylic acid is converted to an ethyl ester using ethanol and a catalytic acid.

* **Procedure:** *p*-Nitrobenzoic acid (1.67 g, 10 mmol) is dissolved in 20 mL of absolute ethanol. 2 mL of concentrated H₂SO₄ is added slowly. The solution is refluxed for 1.5 hours. Upon cooling, the mixture is poured into ice water and neutralized with NaHCO₃ to precipitate the ester.
* **Reference:** *Journal of Chemical Education*, 1999, 76, 11, 1551.
* **DOI:** [10.1021/ed076p1551](https://www.google.com/search?q=https://doi.org/10.1021/ed076p1551)

---

### Step 3: Reduction of the Nitro Group to Benzocaine

The nitro group is reduced to a primary amine. While  (our previous topic) is generally too weak for nitro reduction without a catalyst, tin(II) chloride or catalytic hydrogenation is typically used.

* **Procedure:** Ethyl 4-nitrobenzoate (1.95 g, 10 mmol) is treated with SnCl₂·2H₂O (11.2 g, 50 mmol) in 20 mL of ethanol. The mixture is heated to 70°C for 30 minutes. After cooling, the pH is adjusted to 8-9 with NaOH, and the product is extracted with ethyl acetate.
* **Yield:** ~80-85% across the final step.
* **Reference:** *Organic Syntheses*, Coll. Vol. 2, p.299.
* **DOI:** [10.15227/orgsyn.013.0008](https://doi.org/10.15227/orgsyn.013.0008)
